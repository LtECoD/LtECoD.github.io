The Backdoor Adjustment

  1. do-operator
  2. Backdoor Criterion
  3. Backdoor Adjustment
    1. Relation to Potential Outcomes

Compare to the potential outcomes framework in causal inference, which measures the causal effect with three assumptions, another framework of causal inference, namely structural causal model, exploits structural graphs to model causality. This post gives a concise description to several foundations of the structural causal model, including the do-operator and the backdoor adjustment.

do-operator

Intervention is critical for causal inference since it can help us answer causal questions such as "what if ...". Then how to represent intervention in math? The potential outcomes framework uses Y(t)Y(t)Y(t) to denote what the outcome could be if you were to take treatment ttt. This denotation is different with the observed outcome YYY since not all potential outcomes has been observed. Structural causal model adopts another different but similar denotation for intervention, i.e., do-operator. Specifically, do(t)do(t)do(t) indicates intervention with treatment ttt. It is equivalent to Y(t)Y(t)Y(t) in the potential outcomes framework. For example, we can write the distribution of potential outcome Y(t)Y(t)Y(t) as:

P(Y(t)=y)≜P(Y=y∣do(T=t))≜P(y∣do(t)). \text{P}(Y(t)=y) \triangleq \text{P}(Y=y|do(T=t)) \triangleq \text{P}(y|do(t)). P(Y(t)=y)≜P(Y=y∣do(T=t))≜P(y∣do(t)).

Equation (1) displays an novel denotation P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)). At first glance, we could take it as a conditional probability:

P(y∣do(t))=P(y,do(t))P(do(t)). \text{P}(y|do(t)) = \frac{\text{P}(y, do(t))}{\text{P}(do(t))}. P(y∣do(t))=P(do(t))P(y,do(t))​.

Actually, Equation (2) makes no sense, because both P(y,do(t))\text{P}(y, do(t))P(y,do(t)) and P(do(t))\text{P}(do(t))P(do(t)) are meaningless. Therefore, P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) is not conditional probability at all, it is an atom concept, which just adopts the same denotation "|" as conditional probability.

Then how can we understand the mathematical implication of P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t))? Since dododo-operator comes from structural causal models, we take the causal graph as an entry for figuring out P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)). Suppose we have a three variables, XXX, TTT, and YYY, the causal relation of them is shown in the left of the Figure (1). If we intervene them with treatment T=tT=tT=t, it means that we have manually cut the causal relationship between its parents and TTT. Therefore, the causal graph experiences two changes and we get the causal graph shown in the right of Figure (1). First, the treatment variable TTT is deterministic. Second, the edge representing causal relation from its parents to the treatment variable TTT is removed.

Backdoor Criterion

Similar as the potential outcomes framework that turns causal estimand into statistical estimand (with assumptions: positivity, conditional exchangeability or unconfoundeness, and no interference, see blog potential outcomes):

E[Y(T=1)−Y(T=0)]=EX[E[Y∣t=1,X]−E[Y∣t=0,X]]. \mathbb{E}[Y(T=1)-Y(T=0)]=\mathbb{E}_X\left[\mathbb{E}\left[Y|t=1,X]-\mathbb{E}[Y|t=0,X\right]\right]. E[Y(T=1)−Y(T=0)]=EX​[E[Y∣t=1,X]−E[Y∣t=0,X]].

The structural causal model also aims to turn P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) into a statistical estimand. Although we can achieve this goal with truncated factorization (Proposition 1):

P(y∣do(t))=∑x1,x2,⋯ ;xi≠yP(x1,x2,⋯ ,y∣do(t)), \text{P}(y|do(t))=\sum_{x_1,x_2,\cdots; x_i\not= y}\text{P}(x_1,x_2,\cdots,y|do(t)), P(y∣do(t))=x1​,x2​,⋯;xi​=y∑​P(x1​,x2​,⋯,y∣do(t)),

not all background variables can be observed. Then what variables being observed will make P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) identifiable? Making P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) identifiable means there are only causal relation from treatment variable to the outcome variable. We know that the association between node pairs in the causal graph can only be causal or non-causal. The path that non-causal relation can flow between node pairs is called backdoor path:

Definition 1: (Backdoor Path) They are non-directed unblocked paths from TTT to YYY because there are non-causal association can go in the backdoor of TTT.

If we block all backdoor paths, the remain path from TTT to YYY are causal path. Now we can answer the above question: if some variables being observed can help us block all backdoor paths between TTT and YYY, we could turn P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) into a statistical estimand. We declare that these variables satisfy the backdoor criterion if added with another condition:

Definition 2: (Backdoor Criterion) A set of variables WWW relative to TTT and YYY satisfies the following conditions:

  1. WWW block all backdoor paths from TTT to YYY;

  2. WWW does not contain any descendants of TTT.

An example is shown in the left of Figure (2), W1−3,7W_{1-3,7}W1−3,7​ is the maximum variable set satisfying the backdoor criterion. Actually, any single variable of W1−3W_{1-3}W1−3​ (with or without W7W_7W7​) also satisfies the backdoor criterion. However, anyone of W4−6W_{4-6}W4−6​ being observed will make backdoor criterion not hold.

Backdoor Adjustment

If a set variables WWW, which satisfies the backdoor criterion, are observed, we can turn the causal estimand P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) into a statistical estimand. First, we can margin P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) as:;

P(y∣do(t))=∑wP(y∣w,do(t))P(w∣do(t)) \text{P}(y|do(t)) = \sum_{w}\text{P}(y|w, do(t))\text{P}(w|do(t)) P(y∣do(t))=w∑​P(y∣w,do(t))P(w∣do(t))

This equation omits an intermediate procedure: P(w,y∣do(t))=P(y∣w,do(t))P(w∣do(t))\text{P}(w, y|do(t))=\text{P}(y|w, do(t))\text{P}(w|do(t))P(w,y∣do(t))=P(y∣w,do(t))P(w∣do(t)). You may doubt whether this equation holds. Recall how we explain the mathematical implication of dododo-operator. It just indicates incoming edges of TTT are removed. Therefore, vertical line "|" in P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) doesn't imply conditional probability, as P(y,do(t))\text{P}(y,do(t))P(y,do(t)) and P(do(t))\text{P}(do(t))P(do(t)) don't make sense. Furthermore, P(y∣w,do(t))\text{P}(y|w, do(t))P(y∣w,do(t)) just mix two meanings of ∣|∣. So we can conclude that dododo-operator will not influence the marginalizing out of P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)).

We have supposed WWW has been observed and satisfies the backdoor criterion. The relation between TTT and YYY remains only causal relation. We can easily get

P(y∣w,do(t))=P(y∣w,t). \text{P}(y|w, do(t))=\text{P}(y|w, t). P(y∣w,do(t))=P(y∣w,t).

Since WWW contains no descendants of TTT, intervention on TTT has no influence to WWW. Therefore,

P(w∣do(t))=P(w). \text{P}(w|do(t))=\text{P}(w). P(w∣do(t))=P(w).

Now, we have turned P(y∣do(t))\text{P}(y|do(t))P(y∣do(t)) into a statistical estimand. It is well known as the backdoor adjustment:

Theorem 1: (Backdoor Adjustment) Give modularity (Assumption (1)), WWW satisﬁes the backdoor criterion (Definition 2) and positivity, we can identify the causal effect of TTT on YYY:

P(y∣do(t))=∑wP(y∣w,t)P(w) \text{P}(y|do(t)) = \sum_{w}\text{P}(y|w, t)\text{P}(w) P(y∣do(t))=w∑​P(y∣w,t)P(w)

Relation to Potential Outcomes

Recall the statistical estimand of potential outcomes (Equation (4)) and structural causal model (Equation (9)). Suppose XXX satisfies the backdoor criterion, let us compute the mean potential outcomes YYY after intervention on TTT:

E[Y∣do(t)]=∑yyP(y∣do(t))=∑yy∑wP(y∣x,t)P(x)=EX[E[Y∣X,t]]. \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[Y|do(t)] &= \sum_{y}y\text{P}(y|do(t)) \\ &= \sum_{y}y\sum_{w}\text{P}(y|x, t)\text{P}(x) \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{X}[\mathbb{E}[Y|X,t]]. \end{aligned} E[Y∣do(t)]​=y∑​yP(y∣do(t))=y∑​yw∑​P(y∣x,t)P(x)=EX​[E[Y∣X,t]].​

Then we look at the difference between different intervention with T=1T=1T=1 and T=0T=0T=0:

E[Y∣do(T=1)]−E[Y∣do(T=0)]=EX[E[Y∣X,t=1]−E[Y∣X,t=0]]. \mathbb{E}[Y|do(T=1)] - \mathbb{E}[Y|do(T=0)] = \mathbb{E}_{X}[\mathbb{E}[Y|X,t=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y|X,t=0]]. E[Y∣do(T=1)]−E[Y∣do(T=0)]=EX​[E[Y∣X,t=1]−E[Y∣X,t=0]].

We can find it is equivalent with Equation (4). However, in potential outcomes framework, we don't know how to choose WWW and if conditional exchangeability holds. While in the structural causal model, we can simply choose those variables that satisfies the backdoor criterion.
