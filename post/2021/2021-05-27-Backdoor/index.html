Compare to the potential outcomes framework in causal inference, which measure the causal effect with three assumptions, another framework of causal inference, namely structural causal model, exploits structural graphs to model causality. This post will give a concise description to several foundations of the structural causal model, including the do-operator and the backdoor adjustment.

do-operator

Intervention is critical for causal inference since it can help us answer causal questions such as "what if ...". Then how to represent intervention in math? The potential outcomes framework uses Y(t)Y(t)Y(t) to denote what the outcome could be if you were to take treatment ttt. This denotation is different with the observed outcome YYY since not all potential outcomes has been observed. Structural causal model adopts another different but similar denotation for intervention, i.e., do-operator. Specifically, do(t)do(t)do(t) indicates intervention with treatment ttt. Note that, Y(t)Y(t)Y(t) is equivalent to Y∣do(t)Y|do(t)Y∣do(t), and they can be used on both individual level and population level. For example, we can write the distribution of potential outcome Y(t)Y(t)Y(t) as:

P(Y(t)=y)≜P(Y=y∣do(T=t))≜P(y∣do(t)) \text{P}(Y(t)=y) \triangleq \text{P}(Y=y|do(T=t)) \triangleq \text{P}(y|do(t)) P(Y(t)=y)≜P(Y=y∣do(T=t))≜P(y∣do(t))
